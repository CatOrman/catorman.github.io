SEIZURE DISORDERS

Epilepsy

A central nervous system disorder in which nerve cell activity in the brain becomes disrupted, causing seizures or periods of unusual behavior, sensations and sometimes loss of consciousness.

Symptoms

Recurrent seizures.

Cause

Genetic influence with possible environmental triggers. Epilepsy can also be caused by stroke, brain tumors, prenatal injury, developmental disorders, or infectious diseases such as meningitis, AIDS or viral encephalitis.

Prognosis and Duration

Epilepsy may be a lifelong disorder or may only affect the patient in childhood.

Demographics

1 in 26 Americans will have some kind of seizure disorder in their lifetime. Onset is most common during early childhood or after age 60. Risk factors include family history, head injuries, stroke or vascular diseases, dementia, brain infections or seizures due to high fevers in childhood.

Diagnosis

Neurological examination, blood test for infection, EEG, possible CT scan, MRI or PET scans, or neuropsychological testing.

Treatment

Carbamazepine (Carbatrol and Tegretol) is the first choice for partial, generalized tonic clonic and mixed seizures. Side effects include fatigue, vision changes, nausea, dizziness and rash. Tranquilizers like Diazepam (Valium), lorazepam (Ativan) and Clonazepam (Klonopin) are often used for short term treatment of all seizures or status epilepticus-- however, tolerance develops within a few weeks. Side effects include tiredness, unsteady walking, nausea, depression and loss of appetite in adults and drooling and hyperactivity in children. Alternative therapies include vagus nerve stimulation, which usually reduces seizures by 20-40%, a ketogenic diet, or deep brain stimulation.

Tonic-Clonic Seizure

Seizure activity throughout the entire brain causing loss of consciousness and thrashing. Includes an aura and postictal phase.

Symptoms

Stiffening, loss of consciousness,falling on the ground, eyes roll back into head and back arches. (tonic phase). followed by muscle spasming: elbows, legs and head flex and relax rapidly. loss of consciousness and memory.

Cause

Epilepsy, electrolyte imbalance, infection, drug use.

Prognosis and Duration

Episodes last between one and five minutes.

Demographics

Tonic clonic seizures can affect anyone, but are most common in those with epilepsy.

Diagnosis

EEG, CT or MRI to confirm and search for cost, but most commonly determined by symptoms.

Treatment

Long-term management through medication. Options include Carbamazepine (First choice for partial, generalized tonic clonic and mixed seizures. Side effects: fatigue, vision changes, nausea, dizziness or rash.), Oxcarbazepine (a daily medication to control all seizures. Side effects: Sleepiness, dizziness, headache, vomiting, double vision, balance problems), Phenytoin (for partial and generalized tonic clonic seizures or stopping active seizures by IV) or Perampanel (For partial, primary generalized tonic clonic seizures in those 12 and older).

Absence Seizure

Generalized seizure activity causes a lapse in consciousness without falling or thrashing.

Symptoms

Sudden stop in motion without falling, lip smacking, eyelid flutters, chewing motions, finger rubbing, small movements of both hands, vacant stare. loss of consciousness and memory.

Cause

Epilepsy in adults, idiopathology in children.

Prognosis and Duration

Episodes last between 10 and 30 seconds.

Demographics

2 in 1000 people will experience absence seizures in their lifetimes. Absence seizures do not disproportionately affect one ethnic or geographical group.

Diagnosis

EEG and analysis of symptoms.

Treatment

Ethosuximide (Zarontin) for absence seizures. Side effects include nausea, vomiting, decreased appetite and weight loss. Valproate (Depakene) can treat absence, partial or generalized tonic seizures. Side effects include dizziness, nausea, vomiting, tremor, hair loss, weight gain, depression in adults, irritability in children, reduced attention, cognitive slowness. Long term bone thinning, ankle swelling, irregular menstruation, and rarely, hearing loss, liver damage, decreased platelet counts, pancreas problems.

Myoclonic Seizure

Rapid jerks of a muscle or group of muscles caused by abnormal electrical activity in the brain.

Symptoms

Cause

Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy: characterized by seizures in the neck, shoulders and upper arms which occur most often soon after waking up and usually begin around puberty or early adulthood.

Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome: an uncommon cause of myoclonic seizure in the neck, shoulders, upper arms and face which usually includes other seizure types as well as impaired intellectual development. Myoclonic seizures caused by Lennox-Gastaut are usually stronger and more difficult to control.

Progressive Myoclonic Epilepsy: a rare syndrome characterized by myoclonic as well as tonic-clonic seizures.

Prognosis and Duration

Varies. Does not affect life expectancy.

Demographics

Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy affects 1 in 100,000 Americans.

Diagnosis

Medical history and EEG.

Treatment

No first aid is needed. Medication options include Zonisamide (Zonegran) which is used in combination with other drugs to treat myoclonic, generalized and partial seizures. Side effects include drowsiness, dizziness, unsteady gait, kidney stones, abdominal discomfort, headache, and rash. Levetiracetam (Keppra) may also be used in combination with other epilepsy drugs to treat partial seizures, primary generalized seizures and myoclonic seizures. Oxcarbazepine or tranquilizers may also be used.

Atonic Seizure

A generalized seizure causing the muscles of the body to suddenly relax.

Symptoms

Muscles suddenly go limp and the patient’s body suddenly slumps or crumples to the ground.

Cause

Epilepsy, Lennox-Gastaut syndrome.

Prognosis and Duration

Episodes are usually brief but atonic seizures can be a lifelong condition.

Demographics

Risk factors include family history, head injuries, stroke or vascular diseases, dementia, brain infections or seizures due to high fevers in childhood.

Diagnosis

EEG, medical history

Treatment

Nerve stimulation, ketogenic diet, surgery.

Simple Partial Seizure

Abnormal electrical activity in a small part of the brain causing twitching or a change in sensation, such as a strange taste or smell.

Symptoms

Motor simple partial seizure: jerking motions of the foot, face, arm, or other part of the body.

Sensory simple partial seizure: hearing problems, possible hallucinations, sensory distortions.

Autonomic: changes in blood pressure, heart rhythm, bowel function, etc.

Psychic: feelings of fear, anxiety, deja vu, or other sudden unexplained emotions.

Cause

Epilepsy, brain tumors, brain infections, heat stroke, low blood sugar.

Prognosis and Duration

Dependent on the cause.

Demographics

People with epilepsy, brain tumors, brain infections, heat stroke or low blood sugar.

Diagnosis

Medical history, EEG.

Complex Partial Seizure

Abnormal electrical activity affecting a small part of the brain causing confusion and unresponsiveness for a few minutes.

Symptoms

Staring blankly into space, non-purposeful, repetitive movements, often preceded by a simple partial seizure (aura).

Cause

Epilepsy, brain tumors, brain infection, heat stroke, low blood sugar.

Prognosis and Duration

Staring blankly into space, non-purposeful, repetitive movements, often preceded by a simple partial seizure (aura).

Demographics

Risk factors include epilepsy, brain tumors, brain infection, heat stroke, low blood sugar.

Diagnosis

Medical history, EEG.

Secondary Generalized Seizure

Abnormal electrical activity that begins in one part of the brain but then spreads to both sides of the brain.

Symptoms

Beginning: aura/simple partial seizure or complex partial seizure (see above).

Secondary generalized tonic phase: stiffening of the muscles, cry or groaning sound, loss of consciousness, falling, possible bluishness or tongue biting.

Secondary generalized clonic phase: arms and legs jerk rapidly and rhythmically.

Recovery/consciousness returning: bladder or bowel control may be lost. Consciousness returns slowly. The person may be drowsy, confused, agitated or depressed.

Cause

Epilepsy, head injury, stroke, atherosclerosis in the brain, brain tumor, Alzheimer's Disease, alcohol or drug abuse or withdrawal.

Prognosis and Duration

1-3 minutes of seizing followed by a recovery period.

Demographics

People with head injuries, atherosclerosis, brain tumors, epilepsy, alzheimer’s disease, alcohol or drug abuse or withdrawal.

Diagnosis

Medical history, EEG.

Treatment
