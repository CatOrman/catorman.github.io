Book Review:

This Is Your Brain on Parasites

By Kathleen McAuliffe

This book taught me that spicy foods are more common in hot countries because their antibacterial properties prevent the meats they season from rotting. It explored how toxoplasmosis makes women more social and men more brooding. It doesn't stick neatly to one theme, and instead jumps around to and from loosely related fun facts like those. A great, light read.

By Donald McNeil Jr.

I didn't include Zika on this page because I did my research before the South American outbreak brought it to prominence, but reading this book made me want to take another look. McNeil, a health journalist, provides an inside look at how the Brazillian crisis unfolded and where it might head.

By Dr. Ali S. Khan

A prominent epidemiologist tells the story of little-known but frightening diseases from rabies to Rift Valley Fever. It very nicely combines anecdotes with history with hard science, and proposes solutions rather than just scaring the reader with a problem.

Rabies

A fatal infectious viral disease that causes insanity, rage and convulsions, usually transmitted from mammal to mammal with an animal bite.

Symptoms

Fever, headache, nausea, vomiting, anxiety, confusion, hyperactivity, muscle weakness, difficulty swallowing, hydrophobia, excessive salivation, hallucinations, insomnia, partial paralysis, tingling and burning at the bite site, certain death.

Treatment

Rabies immunoglobulin, given in a series of 5 injections over 14 days, can be administered before the onset of symptoms. Possible side effects of rabies immunoglobulin include headache, nausea, stomachache, muscle ache, dizziness and pain, swelling or itching at the injection site.

After the onset of symptoms, hospice care should be given as the patient will die within a few days

Incidence

Rabies causes 2-3 deaths in the United States per year, 99% of which were transmitted by dogs

Diagnosis

Blood test after the onset of symptoms

Cause

A bullet-shaped rhabdovirus transmitted through saliva into the bloodstream, where it travels up nerve pathways to attack the brain.

Bacterial Encephalitis

Inflammation of the brain caused by a bacterial infection

Symptoms

Headache, fever, muscle aches, fatigue, weakness, confusion, seizures, agitation, and possible hallucinations, loss of sensation, paralysis, muscle weakness, double vision, perception of foul smells, loss of hearing, aphasia, and loss of consciousness.

Cause

Any bacterial infection in the central nervous system.

Diagnosis

Blood tests, lumbar punctures and MRIs can be used to identify brain swelling and the presence of bacteria in the CNS.

Treatment

Antibiotics. The specific drug depends on the bacteria.

Demographics

Young children and the elderly are most likely to be infected.

Viral Encephalitis

Inflammation of the brain caused by a viral infection

Symptoms

Fever, sore throat, cause, malaise, confusion, disorientation, delusions, and possibly hallucinations, agitation, personality changes, aphasia, paralysis, tremors, seizures, and bulging fontaneslles (in infants).

Duration

Incubation periods range from 4-15 days, depending on the cause. The disease usually resolves with treatment within weeks, however untreated encephalitis can take several months to kill.

Causes

Viral encephalitis can becaused by Herpesviruses, including Herpes Simplex Virus 1, Herpes Simplex Virus 2, Epstein-Barr Virus, Varicella-Zoster Virus, or Cytomegalovirus. HSV1 is responsible for most cases of adult herpes encephalitis (5-10% of total encephalitis cases), and tends to be most severe in children and the elderly. HSV2 causes most cases of infant encephalitis, as well as genital herpes.

Mosquito borne Arboviruses are another common cause. These forms of viral encephalitis include La Crosse, West Nile, St. Louis, Western Equine, Eastern Equine, and Powassan. The incubation period, mortality rate, geographic incidence and high-risk population for a given type of encephalitis varies based on the cause. For instance, St. Louis Encephalitis has a mortality rate between 5 and 30%, whereas Western Equine Encephalitis only kills 3-4% of its victims.

Adenoviruses such as measles, mumps and rubella, as well as Enterooviruses such as Polio and Coxsackievirus can also cause encephalitis, but these are now rare.

Demographics

Adults ages 20-40 are at highest risk for Herpesvirus encephalitis, but young children and the elderly are more vulnerable to every other type.

Diagnosis

CT or MRI to look for swelling or other causes of symptoms, LP to test for presence of virus in the central nervous systems, an EEG and blood or urine test for viruses.

Treatment

Antiviral drugs: usually Acyclovir (Zovirax), but sometimes Ganciclovir (Cytovene) or Foscarnet (Foscavir). Antiviral treatment is accompanied by bedrest, plenty of fluids, over-the-counter pain medications and anti-inflammatories.

Bacterial Meningitis

Inflammation of the tissue layers that surround the brain caused by a bacterial infection.

Symptoms

Sudden high fever, unusual severe headache, stiff neck, nausea, vomiting, confusion, difficulty concentrating, seizures, sleepiness, difficulty waking, light sensitivity, loss of appetite, lack of thirst, and possible skin rash.

Cause

Among newborns: Group B Streptococcus, Escheria coli, or Listeria monocytogenes.

Among infants and children: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis or Haemophilus influenzae type B.

Among adolescents and young adults: Streptococcus pneumoniae or neisseria meningitidis.

Among older adults: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, or Listeria monocytogenes.

Incidence

There are 3,000-5,000 cases of bacterial meningitis per year in the United States.

Demographics & Risk Factors

Infants and the immunocompromised are at a higher risk. Individuals who travel to sub-Saharan Africa or to Mecca during Hajj are also at particular risk of infection, as are people living in close quarters such as college dorms or military bunkers.

Diagnosis

A lumbar puncture and blood tests can confirm bacterial meningitis.

Treatment

Viral Meningitis

Inflammation of the tissue layers that surround the brain caused by a viral infection.

Symptoms

Fever, headache, stiff neck, light sensitivity, drowsiness, sleep disturbances, nausea, vomiting, lack of appetite, and lethargy.

Cause

Non-Polio Enteroviruses, Herpesviruses, Arboviruses and Influenza viruses can all cause encephalitis, as can the Mumps and Lymphocytic Choriomeningitis Virus.

Demographics

Viral meningitis is usually seen in children under the age of 5 or people with weakened immune systems.

Diagnosis

Naso-oropharyngeal swabs, rectal swabs, lumbar punctures, and blood and serum tests can all confirm a diagnosis of viral meningitis.

Treatment

Most patients recover on their own within 7-10 days. If the meningitis is caused by a herpesvirus or influenza virus, antivirals like Acyclovir may be required.

Nonparalytic Poliomyelitis

A Poliovirus infection of the central nervous system that produces only mild symptoms and does not result in paralysis.

Symptoms

Fever, sore throat, headache, vomiting, fatigue, pain or stiffness in the neck, back and limbs, muscle weakness and tenderness, and meningitis.

Cause

Infection with Poliovirus.

Demographics & Risk Factors

People who have travelled to an area with a recent polio outbreak or worked with the virus in a lab are at significantly higher risk. In addition, having removed tonsils or being under extreme stress makes people more susceptible to poliomyelitis.

Diagnosis

A physical examination, throat swab, stool sample and lumbar puncture are all used to diagnose nonparalytic poliomyelitis.

Treatment

Rest and painkillers for headaches, muscle aches and muscle spasms.

Paralytic Poliomyelitis

A Poliovirus infection of the central nervous system that produces only mild symptoms and causes temporary or permanent paralysis.

Symptoms

Fever, sore throat, headache, vomiting, fatigue, pain or stiffness in the back, neck and limbs, muscle tenderness and weakness, meningitis, loss of reflexes, severe muscle aches, and flaccid, often asymmetrical paralysis.

Cause

Infection with Poliovirus.

Demographics

eople who have travelled to an area with a recent polio outbreak or worked with the virus in a lab are at significantly higher risk. In addition, having removed tonsils or being under extreme stress makes people more susceptible to poliomyelitis.

Diagnosis

A physical examination, throat swab, stool sample and lumbar puncture are all used to diagnose paralytic poliomyelitis.

Treatment

Rest, painkillers for headaches, muscle aches and back spasms, and antibiotics for the urinary tract infections that frequently accompany poliomyelitis. As the paralysis progresses, a ventilator may be necessary to assist breathing and physical therapy and braces may be necessary to assist walking.

Tetanus

An infectious bacterial disease marked by rigidity and spasms of the voluntary muscles.

Symptoms

Jaw spasms and stiffness, neck stiffness, abdominal stiffness, difficulty swallowing, painful body spasms, and possible fever, sweating, hypertension and tachycardia.

Cause

The Clostridium tetani bacterium, found in soil, dust and animal feces, enters a deep wound and produces spores which secrete a powerful toxin. This toxin, tetanospasmin, impairs the function of the motor neuron.

Duration & Prognosis

The incubation period ranges from 3 to 21 days, usually about 10 days. The further away the wound site is from the brain and spine, the longer the incubation period.

Demographics

Usually only occurs in those who are not vaccinated or haven't stayed up to date on their booster shots.

Incidence

29 cases per year are reported in the United States.

Diagnosis

Physical examination. No lab test currently exists that can confirm a case of tetanus.

Treatment

The first line of treatment is immediate injection with human tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG) or equine antitoxin. Tetanus toxoid booster, wound care and antibiotics are also necessary. If the infection progresses, mechanical ventilation, tracheostomy or intubation may be required to assist breathing.

Post-Polio Syndrome

A cluster of disabling symptoms that affect some people several years after contracting polio.

Symptoms

Progressive muscle or joint weakness or pain, general fatigue, muscle atrophy, breathing or swallowing difficulty, sleep-related breathing disorders, sensitivity to cold, cognitive difficulty, depression or mood swings.

Cause

A prior poliovirus infection damages the neurons. In response, the neurons sprout new dendrites, and the surviving motor units become enlarged. The stress of so much new input on only one neuron becomes more than it can handle, so the new fibers and eventually, the entire cell deteriorate.

Prognosis and Duration

Post-Polio syndrome is a chronic condition. Onset is at least 15 years after the initial infection.

Risk Factors

People whose polio infection was especially severe, but who recovered especially quickly are more likely to suffer from post-polio syndrome. A high level of physical activity is also a risk factor.

Diagnosis

Electromyography and nerve conduction studies are done to rule out neuropathy and myopathy. MRIs or CT scans can rule out spinal disorders, spondylosis and spinal stenosis. Following these tests, a muscle biopsy and blood testing will assist in diagnosis of post-polio syndrome.

Treatment

Treatment is focused on alleviating symptoms. Physical, occupational and speech therapy, as well as lots of rest, can assist in movement difficulties. Medications such as ibuprofen, acetaminophen and aspirin ease muscle and joint pain.

Brain Abscess

A collection of pus and inflamed tissue that can be found in or around the brain, usually caused by a bacterial infection.

Symptoms

Headache, fever, nausea, vomiting, stiff neck, cognitive difficulty, decreased movement, changes in personality or vision, drowsiness, confusion, seizures. possible: speech difficulties, vision problems, and limb weakness. In infants, bulging fontanelles, projectile vomiting, high-pitched crying and spasticity in the limbs are often seen.

Cause

Inflammation (infection) and collection of infected material. Common causative bacteria include Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, Bacteroides and Listeria species. Fungi such as Aspergillus, Candida, Cryptococcus, Coccidioides, Histoplasma and Blastomyces species, as well as protozoa like T. gondii, E. histolytica, T. cruzi and Schistosoma can also cause abscesses.

Prognosis

Mortality ranges between 17% and 32%. Patients with infection in the basal ganglia or thalamus, or intraventricular rupture suffer higher rates of mortality.

Demographics

Brain abscesses are more often found in those with a compromised immune system, cancer or another chronic illness, congenital heart disease, meningitis, a major head injury, or chronic sinus and middle ear infections. Most occur in developing countries and low-income settings.

Diagnosis

A CT, MRI or lumbar puncture can confirm the presence of a brain abscess. A lumbar puncture cannot be done if brain swelling is suspected.

Treatment

Antibiotics are the first line of treatment, but if the brain abscess contains gases or is likely to rupture, surgery may be necessary.
