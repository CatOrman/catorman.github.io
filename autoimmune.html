Relapsing/Remitting Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis is an autoimmune illness that causes the fatty sheaths surrounding the axons of neurons in the brain and spine to erode. This type is the most common, and is defined by a symptom progression that cycles between getting worse and getting better.

Symptoms

Limb numbness, limb weakness, trunk numbness, trunk weakness, partial or complete loss of vision, usually in one eye at a time, pain during eye movement, prolonged double vision, tingling, pain, Lhermitte's sign, tremor, lack of coordination, unsteady gait, slurred speech, fatigue, dizziness, and incontinence.

Cause

The autoimmune system attacks the myelin sheaths around the axons of neurons in the brain and spinal cord. This is strongly linked to vitamin D deficiency, which is why incidence increases with latitude. In fact, the first case ever described was in a famous Dutch holy woman who fell while ice skating in 1395. Studies suggest that there are a combination of genetic and environmental factors, as well as certain viruses (epstein-barr, varicella zoster, the hepatitis vaccine) which are associated with the disease.

Prognosis

Life expectancy is only slightly shortened, but MS is a chronic disease that currently has no cure. As the name suggests, patients go into remission repeatedly, but each time the disease returns it gets more severe.

Risk Factors

MS mostly affects white people by the numbers, but darker-skinned people living at high latitudes are at the highest risk. The average age of onset is 34, and the disease is more common in males. Incidence increases with distance from the equator.

Diagnosis

An MRI scan, a lumbar puncture for immunoglobulin levels, a standard neurological examination and a patient history are all necessary.

Treatment

Corticosteroids to reduce nerve inflammation. This includes oral prednisone and IV methylprednisone. Side effects are insonmia, hypertension, mood swings, and increased fluid retention.

Plasmapheresis is recommended for patients with new, more severe symptoms that are unresponsive to steroid treatment. Beta interferon for viral activity is also an option, with the side effects of flu-like symptoms, injection site reactions, and liver damage. Glatiramer Acetate (Copaxone) can help block immune responses in the myelin sheaths, and Dimethyl Fumarate (Tecfidera) can reduce the relapse rate in relapsing/remitting MS.

Symptomatic treatments include physical therapy, muscle relaxants for spasming and stiffness, and a variety of medications for fatigue, depression, pain, sexual dysfunction and incontinence.

Myesthenia Gravis

A chronic autoimmune neuromuscular disease causing weakness in the skeletal muscles that improves after rest and worsens during physical activity.

Risk Factors

MG affects all ethnic groups and sexes. Women under 40 and men over 60 are most commonly diagnosed, but any age group can be affected.

Symptoms

Early on: Weakness in eye muscles, difficulty swallowing or slurred speech. Onset can be sudden.

Later: ptosis (drooping of eyelids), diplopia, unstable or waddling gait, altered facial expression, difficulty swallowing, shortness of breath, dysarthria, and weakness in the arms, hands, fingers, legs or neck.

Cause

Antibodies block, destroy or alter the acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, preventing muscle contractions. The thymus gland, which develops the immune system early in life, stays large even though in normal individuals it shrinks with maturity. It most likely gives false instructions to the antibodies and causes the interruption at the neuromuscular junction.

Prognosis

Myasthenia Gravis is a chronic disease, but life expectancy remains the same.

Diagnosis

Edrophonium test: IV edrophonium chloride can very briefly restore strength to MG positive patient.

Single fiber electromyography to detect impaired nerve-to-muscle transmission (EMG)

Blood test for elevated levels of acetylcholine receptor antibodies and anti-muSK antibody (not 100% of patients test positive)

A standard neurological and physical examination is also performed. Physicans look for impairments in eye movement that appear without a deficit in feeling.

Treatment

Medications: anticholinesterase agents such as pyridostigmine and neostigmine block the neurotransmitter acetylcholine, which controls voluntary skeletal movement. High doses of corticosteroids like prednisone, azathioprine, cyclosporin, mycophenolate mofetil, and tacrolimus suppress the antibody response.

Other therapies include thymectomy, especially for patients with thymoma. Plasmapheresis is used to remove serum with abnormal antibodies, which the body replaces with normal cells. High-dose IV immunoglobulin is prescribed during periods of the most pronounced weakness to modulate the autoimmune response.
