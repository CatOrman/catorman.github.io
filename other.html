Neurofibromatosis Type I

A genetic disorder that causes benign tumors to form throughout the nervous system. Type I usually appears between birth and age 10 with mild to moderate severity.

Symptoms

Cafe au lait spots on the skin, usually more than 6, which appear during the first years of life, Lisch nodules (tiny harmless bumps on the iris), neurofibromas (soft bumps under the skin that turn out to be nerve tumors), bone deformities like scoliosis, optic glioma usually appearing by age 3, learning disabilities, large head, short stature.

Cause

A mutation in gene on chromosome 17 that produces neurofibromin, causing it to overproduce and allow cells to grow uncontrollably.

Prognosis and Duration

Life expectancy is normal or close to normal.

Demographics

Risk factors are limited to heritability.

Diagnosis

An eye exam for Lisch nodules and cataracts, audiometry, electronystagmography and brainstem auditory evoked response to test for hearing and balance difficulty present in Neurofibromatosis type II, genetic testing for neurofibromatosis types I and II, and an X-ray, CT and MRI for bone abnormalities and tumors.

Treatment

Main treatment is monitoring of symptoms. Surgical options include tumor removal for neurofibromas that are compressing tissue or damaging organs.

Neurofibromatosis Type II

A genetic disorder that causes benign tumors to form throughout the nervous system. Type II appears in late adolescence or young adulthood and varies in severity.

Symptoms

Initial bilateral acoustic neuroma or vestibular schwannoma leads to gradual hearing loss, tinnitus, balance problems, and headaches. Possible: schwannomas in other nerves, numbness in limbs, weakness in limbs, pain, balance problems, facial droop, vision problems, cataracts.

Cause

A mutation in gene on chromosome 22 that produces merlin, causing it to underproduce and and allow cells to grow uncontrollably.

Prognosis and Duration

Life expectancy is normal or close to normal.

Demographics

Risk factors are limited to heritability.

Diagnosis

An eye exam for Lisch nodules and cataracts, audiometry, electronystagmography and brainstem auditory evoked response to test for hearing and balance difficulty present in Neurofibromatosis type II, genetic testing for neurofibromatosis types I and II, and an X-ray, CT and MRI for bone abnormalities and tumors.

Treatment

Main treatment is monitoring of symptoms. Surgical options include tumor removal for neurofibromas that are compressing tissue or damaging organs, or stereotactic radiosurgery to remove acoustic neuromas. Auditory brainstem implants or cochlear implants may be necessary to improve hearing.

Schwannomatosis

A genetic disorder that causes benign tumors to form throughout the nervous system. This type causes tumors to develop on cranial, spinal and peripheral nerves but not the vestibulocochlear nerve. Onset is usually after age 20.

Symptoms

Chronic pain, muscle loss, numbness, weakness

Cause

SMARCB2 (aka INI1) gene mutation.

Prognosis and Duration

Life expectancy is normal or close to normal.

Demographics

Risk factors are limited to heritability.

Diagnosis

An eye exam for Lisch nodules and cataracts, audiometry, electronystagmography and brainstem auditory evoked response to test for hearing and balance difficulty present in Neurofibromatosis type II, genetic testing for neurofibromatosis types I and II, and an X-ray, CT and MRI for bone abnormalities and tumors.

Treatment

Main treatment is monitoring of symptoms. Surgical options include tumor removal for neurofibromas that are compressing tissue or damaging organs. Pain medications include Gabapentin (Neurotin) or Pregabalin (Lyrica) for nerve pain. Antidepressant options include Duloxetine (Cymbalta) and Amitriptyline. Epileptic medication options for schwannomatosis-induced seizures include Topiramate (Topamax) or Carbamazepine (Carbatrol, Tegretol).

Trigeminal Neuralgia

A chronic pain condition affecting the nerve that perceives facial sensation.

Symptoms

Episodes of severe, shooting or jabbing pain that may feel like an electric shock, spontaneous attacks of pain triggered by touching, chewing or brushing teeth, bouts of pain from a few seconds to several minutes, constant aching or burning, pain in the cheek, jaw, teeth, gums, lips, eye or forehead, unilateral pain, attacks that become more frequent and intense over time.

Cause

The most common cause is a blood vessel pressing on the trigeminal nerve where it exits the brainstem, leading to myelin degeneration. Other causes include nerve compression from a tumor, arteriovenous malformation, multiple sclerosis, or injury to the trigeminal nerve associated with sinus or oral surgery, stroke or facial trauma.

Prognosis and Duration

Chronic.

Demographics

Trigeminal neuralgia is more common in women and people over 50. The incidence of new cases is 12 per 100,000 people per year.

Diagnosis

Medical history, neurological examination, possible MRI to show blood vessel on nerve.

Treatment

Anticonvulsants: Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, topiramate, gabapentin, pregabalin, clonazepam, phenytoin, lamotrigine, and valproic acid. Tricyclic antidepressants: Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline. Surgical options include rhizotomy, which damages nerve fibers to block pain and always causes some level of lost sensation in the face and senses. Methods of rhizotomy include balloon compression, glycerol injection, radiofrequency thermal lesioning, stereotactic radiosurgery or microvascular decompression. Neurectomy (cutting part of the nerve) is also an option.

Glossopharyngeal Neuralgia

A rare pain syndrome affecting the cranial nerve in the neck that causes sharp pulses of pain in the back of the throat, tonsils, middle ear and tongue.

Symptoms

Severe pain in the back of the tongue, ear, throat, tonsil area, larynx, nasopharynx, possibly triggered by chewing, coughing, laughing, speaking, or swallowing.

Cause

Irritation of the glossopharyngeal nerve caused by a blood vessel, growth at the base of the skull, or tumors or infections of the throat and mouth pressing on the nerve.

Prognosis and Duration

Demographics

Symptoms usually begin in people over 40.

Diagnosis

Blood testing for infection, CT or MRI to look for swelling, MRA or CT angiogram to look for blood vessel.

Treatment

Antiseizure medication like Carbamazepine or Gabapentin, antidepressants, or surgery. Surgical options include nerve resection, tractotomy or microvascular decompression.

Postherpetic Neuralgia

Nerve pain in the skin caused by previous damage done by infection with the herpes zoster virus.

Symptoms

In the affected area: intense itchiness, burning, stabbing, shooting, aching, throbbing, hyperalgesia, allodynia.

Cause

A shingles outbreak damages nerve fibers, which impairs signalling to the brain and causes sensory perception to become exaggerated.

Prognosis and Duration

Recurring outbreaks.

Demographics

Postherpetic neuralgia affects people over 50 who have had a shingles outbreak with a severe rash and severe pain, especially if the rash was on the face or torso. People who have another chronic disease such as diabetes are at increased risk.

Diagnosis

Neurological examination.

Treatment

Lidocaine or capsaicin skin patch on the rash, anticonvulsants such as Gabapentin or Pregabalin, antidepressants such as nortriptyline, duloxetine, venlakafine, opioid painkillers such as tramadol, oxycodone and morphine, or steroid injections.
